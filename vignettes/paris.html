The Girl Lost Her Mother In Paris

[110] Of all the stories I know about mothers, this one is the most real. A young American girl is visiting Paris with her mother when the woman begins to feel ill. They decide to check into a hotel for a few days so the mother can rest, and the daughter calls for a doctor to assess her.
[111] After a brief examination, the doctor tells the daughter that all her mother needs is some medicine. He takes the daughter to a taxi, gives the driver directions in French, and explains to the girl that, at his home, his wife will give her the appropriate remedy. They drive and drive for a very long time, and when the girl arrives, she is frustrated by the unbearable slowness of this doctor’s wife, who meticulously assembles the pills from powder. When she gets back into the taxi, the driver meanders down the streets, sometimes doubling back on the same avenue. The girl gets out of the taxi to return to the hotel on foot. When she finally arrives, the hotel clerk tells her that he has never seen her before. When she runs up to the room where her mother had been resting, she finds the walls a different colour, the furnishings different than her memory, and her mother nowhere in sight.
[112] There are many endings to the story. In one of them, the girl is gloriously persistent and certain, renting a room nearby and staking out the hotel, eventually seducing a young man who works in the laundry and discovering the truth: that her mother had died of a contagious and fatal disease, departing this plane shortly after the daughter was sent from the hotel by the doctor. To avoid a citywide panic, the staff removed and buried her body, repainted and furnished the room, and bribed all involved to deny that they had ever met the pair.
[113] In another version of this story, the girl wanders the streets of Paris for years, believing that she is mad, that she invented her mother and her life with her mother in her own diseased mind. The daughter stumbles from hotel to hotel, confused and grieving, though for whom she cannot say.
[114] I don’t need to tell you the moral of this story. I think you already know what it is.

The first version of this story ends with the girl successfully finding out the truth: the ideal ending. The second one ends with everyone successfully gaslighting her, which ends up in her losing her sanity due to extreme self-doubt. This contrast illustrates the precarious dichotomy women face in their pursuit of truth and equality: they are met either with hard-won success or systemic deception. In both scenarios, however, their dignity is compromised. In the first version, the American girl was only able to find out the truth because she seduced a male hotel staff member. This suggests a troubling reality where women’s agency is contingent upon their objectification; the truth is only accessible through the commodification of the female body. Ultimately, the narrative presents a grim "illusion of choice" for women navigating patriarchal structures: be used or be useless.
