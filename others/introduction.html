The Green Ribbon: A Feminist Analysis

“Nice and tight, everyone’s happy.” In a patriarchal society, marriage and motherhood are what make a woman. Ironically, they are expected to conceive children while simultaneously preserving their bodies for their husbands. These seemingly absurd gender roles and expectations pressure women into committing to something sacrificial, leaving them with no autonomy over their bodies, as all parts are left and designated to the men. A manifestation of this system—one that is arguably the most gruesome of all—is in a medical procedure called a "husband stitch." Deemed unnecessary by medical practitioners, this procedure involves tightening the vaginal orifice by adding sutures when repairing the tear caused by childbirth.

In The Husband Stitch by Carmen Maria Machado, she retells the children’s horror story The Green Ribbon. In this short tale originating from the 1800s, a girl named Jenny wears a green ribbon around her neck, the reason for which she refuses to disclose to her childhood friend and eventual husband, Alfred. On her deathbed, Alfred finally learned that the green ribbon binds her head and her shoulders; that is why she never takes off the ribbon (Schwartz 24-33).

In her retelling, Machado features a female protagonist—who also wears a green ribbon—narrating her life as she goes through courtship, marriage, childbearing, childbirth, and parenting. Her story revolves around how her husband used her for pleasure, even at the times she was not aware of and therefore did not consent to, peaking at the performance of “the husband stitch” to her. Through the lens of feminist criticism, The Husband Stitch will be analyzed to highlight themes of women's disempowerment, coercion, and the overall destructive impact of patriarchal governance.

Machado uses a green ribbon as the central motif and as the symbol of the female protagonist’s remaining self-autonomy—the only thing connecting her head to her body. As the story progresses, the protagonist’s husband continues to make attempts at untying the ribbon, implying how the protagonist gradually loses herself to her husband and, eventually, her son. The catastrophic impact of heteronomy on women is further highlighted by vignettes inspired by horror and urban legends centering on their experiences.
