From Raindrops to Puddle

The woman described the urban legends as like raindrops. They are individual during the fall but merge together into a puddle once they land. Each woman has her story—a unique experience that resulted from misogyny that has defined her life significantly. As subalterns in the perspective of the patriarchy, their stories are deemed insignificant and, as such, seldomly heard.

The journey of the green ribbon in Carmen Maria Machado’s The Husband Stitch serves as a chilling map of a woman’s systematic erasure. What originated as a spooky childhood horror story evolves into the grim reality of womanhood, where the supernatural dread of urban legends is eclipsed by the tangible horror of patriarchal entitlement. Through the horror urban legends, Machado shows us that the most pervasive threat to a woman’s agency is not supernatural phenomena but real-life institutionalized dispossession built into marriage and motherhood.

The end of the story—the literal untying of the ribbon—represents the final collapse of the protagonist’s autonomy. In this moment, the protagonist transcends her own life to become just another urban legend in the survival manual she has been narrating. She is no longer the author of her own story; she has become a cautionary tale.

Ultimately, the horror in the woman’s narrative is twofold. It lies in the supernatural image of a severed head and the everyday, systemic violence of a husband who views his wife as an extension of his own authority. The catastrophic impact of heteronomy is clear: patriarchal governance demands a sacrificial self-annihilation. To keep the "puddle" of women’s stories from being ignored, there must be a collective effort to turn these individual raindrops into a flood that breaks down the structures of domestic subordination. True agency can only be reclaimed when we stop expecting women to be "nice and tight" for the comfort of others and finally respect their right to their own untouched ribbons.
