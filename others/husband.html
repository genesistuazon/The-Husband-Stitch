The Husband's Attempts

The husband’s attempt at untying the ribbon spans through the entirety of the story, often featured when her boundaries are being tested. It is first mentioned during their first kiss (6), wherein the girl forbids the boy from touching it. The boy’s compliance shows his acknowledgement of her boundaries, underscoring her continued bodily autonomy. During their second encounter, the boy expressed his desire to touch the ribbon (17-22). Although he initially complies with the girl’s refusal, his subsequent actions reveal a persistent fixation, implying that his compliance is not out of respect for the girl’s decision but a mere suppression of his impulse.

This fixation is not extinguished when the girl, at the beginning of their relationship, establishes two rules: the boy cannot inseminate inside her and he cannot touch the ribbon (32). While she voluntarily rescinds the former on their wedding day, the boy’s nonconsensual touching compromises the latter. It can be inferred by the girl’s passivity of this transgression that she is forced to renegotiate her boundaries now that the boy—soon to be her husband—and she are in a more committed relationship (61). From not touching the ribbon, the rule is compromised to not untie it. This shift represents an erosion of her autonomy in conjunction with her marriage.

Societal norms tend to force women to give up a substantial portion of their bodily authority to their husbands. According to Michel Foucault’s concept of biopower, marriage becomes an institution that facilitates the dispossession of women's agency (Cisney and Morar 2-4). This belief that lingered and dominated over centuries is a huge factor in why the girl deconstructed her boundaries. She rescinded her first rule because now that she and the boy are married, she now carries the responsibility of child-bearing. Patriarchal governance pressures women to “overspecialize” in reproductive labor as compensation for the men’s economic support for the family (Braunstein and Folbre 25-44). Moreover, as acknowledgement of the sanctity of marriage—that the husband and wife are now one—she renegotiated her second rule to allocate some power to her husband while still protecting the integrity of the ribbon.

In a scene where the woman and her husband are discussing raising a child, the husband’s quiet fixation transformed into an abrupt and aggressive attempt at untying the ribbon. This intensification of his desires about the ribbon is heavily influenced by their plan to have a child, because a deeper commitment than marriage is raising a family. What better way to tie a woman tighter to a man after marriage than a child? If the woman becoming her wife is not enough to give him access to the ribbon, a child might. In this case, the woman felt more compelled to obey her husband’s wishes.

Upon the husband’s unrelenting attempts at invading the ribbon, his entitlement was internalized by the woman by the end of the story. Years of her husband’s subtle manipulation in the form of deceitful restraint made it harder for her to realize the oppression and resist (Wilcock 76-101). Finally, she lets him untie the ribbon as proof of her unconditional love to him as she perishes thereafter.

At each attempt of the husband to untie the ribbon, the woman loses a part of her autonomy. This progresses as their commitment to each other intensifies through marriage and child-raising, until eventually, she loses it all. Her head—her cognition, voice of reason, and power—disconnects from her body.
